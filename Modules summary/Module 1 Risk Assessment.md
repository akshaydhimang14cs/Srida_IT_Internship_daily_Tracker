# Module 1: Risk Assessment

## Overview
This module focused on the fundamentals of risk assessment and the structured process used to identify, evaluate, and manage information security risks in an organization. The learning covered key stages such as asset identification, threat identification, vulnerability identification, likelihood and impact determination, risk scoring, risk treatment, documentation, and monitoring.

## Duration Covered
03 June 2026 to 07 June 2026

## Topics Covered

### 1. Identify Assets and Data
The module began with understanding what qualifies as an information asset. Assets include hardware, software, data, people, and business processes that store, process, or transmit information.

Key learning points:
- Information assets are not limited to devices or files; people and business processes are also assets.
- Every asset should have an owner responsible for protection, classification, and lifecycle management.
- Asset classification helps determine sensitivity and required security controls.
- A proper asset register is essential for maintaining visibility of organizational assets.

Asset categories studied:
- Public
- Internal
- Confidential
- Restricted

### 2. Identify Threats
This section focused on identifying threats that could affect organizational assets.

Key learning points:
- A threat is any event or actor that can exploit a vulnerability and cause harm.
- Threats should be analyzed based on source, motivation, and capability.
- Categorizing threats helps improve structured risk analysis and prioritization.
- Threat intelligence supports more informed threat identification.

Threat categories studied:
- Human threats
- Natural threats
- Technical threats
- Environmental threats

Threat modeling concepts studied:
- STRIDE
- PASTA
- OCTAVE
- VAST

### 3. Identify Vulnerabilities
The module then moved to identifying vulnerabilities that may exist across different asset types and control layers.

Key learning points:
- A vulnerability is a weakness in an asset, process, system, or control that can be exploited.
- Vulnerabilities may be technical, operational, physical, or human in nature.
- Vulnerability identification should be linked to asset context for effective prioritization.
- Vulnerability scanning and manual validation both play important roles.

Tools and methods discussed:
- CVE
- CVSS
- Nessus
- Qualys
- OpenVAS
- Burp Suite
- Automated scanning
- Manual testing
- Penetration testing

### 4. Determine Likelihood
This part of the module focused on evaluating how likely a threat is to exploit a vulnerability.

Key learning points:
- Likelihood is the probability of a threat exploiting a vulnerability within a given period.
- Likelihood depends on threat capability, threat motivation, opportunity, and control effectiveness.
- A structured likelihood scale improves consistency and repeatability.
- Historical incidents and current threat intelligence support rating decisions.

### 5. Determine Impact
Impact assessment focused on understanding the consequences if a risk event occurs.

Key learning points:
- Impact represents the severity of harm caused to the organization.
- Impact can be financial, operational, reputational, legal/regulatory, or safety-related.
- Impact analysis should include both direct and cascading consequences.
- Both qualitative and quantitative approaches can be used depending on the situation.

Important concepts discussed:
- ALE
- SLE
- ARO

### 6. Determine Risk Score
This section explained how likelihood and impact are combined to prioritize risks.

Key learning points:
- Risk scoring helps determine which risks require urgent attention.
- Inherent risk is the level of risk before controls are considered.
- Residual risk is the level of risk remaining after controls are applied.
- Risk matrices help classify risks into low, medium, high, and critical levels.

### 7. Compare Score with Risk Appetite
This part focused on comparing calculated risk levels with the organization’s acceptable level of risk.

Key learning points:
- Risk appetite helps determine which risks are acceptable and which require action.
- Risks above acceptable thresholds require treatment or escalation.
- Business tolerance and regulatory expectations influence risk decisions.

### 8. Risk Treatment
This section covered the practical options available to respond to identified risks.

Risk treatment options studied:
- Mitigate
- Avoid
- Transfer
- Accept

Key learning points:
- Risk treatment decisions should align with business objectives and available controls.
- The objective is to reduce risk to an acceptable level, not always to eliminate it completely.

### 9. Document and Monitor
The final section emphasized the importance of documentation and continuous follow-up.

Key learning points:
- Risks, controls, owners, and treatment actions must be documented clearly.
- Risk registers support accountability and review.
- Monitoring ensures that risks remain under control over time.
- Risk assessment is a continuous process, not a one-time activity.

## Overall Learning Outcome
This module provided a complete foundation in the risk assessment lifecycle. It explained how to move from identifying assets, threats, and vulnerabilities to evaluating likelihood, impact, and risk score, followed by treatment, documentation, and monitoring.
