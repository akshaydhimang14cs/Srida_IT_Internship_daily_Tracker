# Module 4: Incident Response and Handling

## Overview
This module provided a complete and practice-oriented understanding of incident response, from foundational concepts and response frameworks to containment, eradication, recovery, post-incident review, compliance reporting, playbook design, tabletop exercises, and maturity improvement. The learning established incident response not as a reactive technical activity alone, but as a structured organizational capability that integrates governance, legal awareness, operational readiness, communication discipline, and continuous improvement.

## Duration Covered
15 June 2026 to 18 June 2026

## Topics Covered

### 1. Incident Response Fundamentals
The module began by defining incident response as the organized approach used to manage the aftermath of a security breach or cyberattack in a way that limits damage, reduces recovery time and cost, and lowers the likelihood of recurrence. It clarified the distinction between a security event and a true security incident, explained why triage is essential, and established the business importance of a formal incident response capability.

This section also introduced the major incident response frameworks, especially NIST SP 800-61 and the SANS PICERL model. The module explained the four-phase NIST structure and the more granular six-phase SANS lifecycle, showing how both frameworks support structured, repeatable, and defensible response operations.

### 2. Incident Response Lifecycle and CSIRT Structure
The module explained incident response as an iterative lifecycle rather than a one-time process. It covered preparation, detection and analysis, containment, eradication and recovery, and post-incident activity, while emphasizing that strong preparation determines the success of all later response phases.

It also introduced the Computer Security Incident Response Team (CSIRT), including team roles, RACI-based responsibility assignment, internal and external participation models, reporting lines, and 24/7 coverage structures. This established the governance and staffing foundation needed for effective response execution.

### 3. Preparation and Planning
This section focused on the policy and planning framework required before an incident occurs. It distinguished among the IR policy, the IR plan, and detailed playbooks, and explained how these documents function together from executive governance down to tactical execution.

The module also covered scope definition, objectives, legal and regulatory obligations, communication planning, escalation matrices, spokesperson designation, tooling strategy, jump bags, asset visibility, log retention, and evidence collection readiness. Together, these topics showed that incident response effectiveness depends heavily on preparation quality, documentation maturity, and infrastructure readiness.

### 4. Detection and Analysis
The module then moved into the operational mechanics of detecting and validating incidents. It covered multi-source detection using IDS/IPS, SIEM, EDR, user reporting, threat intelligence, and network monitoring, while explaining how layered visibility reduces blind spots and improves mean time to detect.

A detailed treatment of triage and severity classification followed, including impact assessment, urgency determination, false-positive handling, and initial incident documentation. The chapter then expanded into analysis techniques such as log review, network traffic analysis, memory forensics, timeline construction, artifact correlation, and root-cause-focused investigation.

### 5. Containment Strategies
This section explained how to select and implement containment measures that stop attacker activity while preserving evidence and minimizing unnecessary business disruption. It distinguished short-term containment from long-term containment and emphasized the principle that containment must be established before eradication begins.

The module covered network isolation, segmentation, firewall rule changes, DNS sinkholing, account lockout procedures, EDR-based endpoint isolation, virtual machine snapshots, and rollback planning. It also gave significant attention to evidence preservation, chain of custody, order of volatility, forensic imaging, and legal admissibility considerations.

### 6. Eradication and Recovery
The eradication and recovery portion of the module focused on removing threats completely and restoring systems in a safe, controlled, and verifiable way. It explained root cause identification, persistence removal, vulnerability remediation, credential resets, API key rotation, and system hardening.

The recovery section then addressed recovery prioritization, backup validation, restoration from trusted sources, golden images, phased recovery, system verification testing, enhanced monitoring, user communication, and formal business sign-off. The module emphasized that recovery quality is more important than recovery speed and that organizations must validate security posture before returning systems fully to production.

### 7. Post-Incident Activity and Lessons Learned
This section taught how to conduct disciplined post-incident review and convert experience into measurable improvement. It covered the purpose and timing of post-incident reviews, the importance of a blameless culture, attendee roles, meeting structure, root cause analysis, documentation requirements, action tracking, plan updates, and knowledge base maintenance.

The module also introduced incident response metrics such as Mean Time to Detect (MTTD), Mean Time to Respond (MTTR), Mean Time to Contain (MTTC), false positive rate, recurrence rate, and action closure rate. It connected these metrics to leadership reporting, program diagnostics, and long-term improvement planning.

### 8. Reporting and Compliance Requirements
The module further addressed reporting obligations that arise when incidents involve personal data breaches, service disruption, or regulated information. It discussed the need to work closely with legal counsel, document the point of awareness, and meet applicable notification timelines under frameworks such as GDPR, DPDPA, HIPAA, PCI DSS, SOX, and NIS2.

This part also covered customer communication, executive and board reporting, law enforcement coordination, and cyber insurance documentation. The emphasis was on building notification readiness in advance so that legal, regulatory, and stakeholder expectations can be met without confusion during a live incident.

### 9. Playbooks and Tabletop Exercises
The final instructional portion of the module explained how to build and maintain effective incident response playbooks. It distinguished the strategic IR plan from tactical playbooks and operational runbooks, then outlined the structure of strong playbooks including triggers, severity guidance, detection steps, containment decision trees, escalation points, eradication actions, recovery tasks, communication templates, and evidence requirements.

The module also explained tabletop exercises as a practical method for testing communication, coordination, decision-making, and role clarity. It covered scenario planning, inject design, facilitator and observer roles, exercise logistics, and after-action reporting.

### 10. Measuring and Improving IR Maturity
The module concluded by presenting maturity models for incident response and the idea of continuous improvement as a formal management discipline. It described the progression from ad hoc response to optimized and metrics-driven programs, and explained how benchmarking, annual review, and repeated improvement cycles help organizations strengthen their incident response capability over time.

This final section reinforced that a mature incident response program is not defined only by tools, but by consistency, governance, tested processes, evidence discipline, learning culture, and measurable improvement.

## Overall Learning Outcome
This module built a complete foundation in incident response and handling. It developed an end-to-end understanding of how organizations prepare for incidents, detect and validate threats, contain attacker activity, preserve evidence, eradicate root causes, restore services, meet regulatory obligations, and improve readiness through structured lessons learned, playbooks, exercises, and maturity assessment.
