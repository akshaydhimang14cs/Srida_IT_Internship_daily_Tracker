# Module 3: Cybersecurity Audit Fundamentals

## Overview
This module provides a complete foundation in cybersecurity auditing, from the meaning and purpose of an audit to the practical discipline of evidence collection, risk assessment, reporting, remediation, continuous monitoring, cloud security, vendor risk, and emerging-threat auditing. It explains how a cybersecurity auditor should think, plan, test, classify, report, and follow through until findings are properly resolved.

## Duration Covered
11 June 2026 to 14 June 2026

## Topics Covered

### 1. Foundations of Cybersecurity Auditing
The module begins by defining a cybersecurity audit as a systematic and independent evaluation of information security controls, policies, and procedures. It distinguishes audits from assessments and penetration tests, explains why organizations conduct audits, and introduces the five phases of the audit lifecycle: planning and scoping, fieldwork and evidence collection, analysis and evaluation, reporting, and follow-up and remediation tracking.

### 2. Governance, Risk, and Compliance Landscape
This section explains the GRC model and its three pillars: governance, risk management, and compliance. It shows how cybersecurity audits sit within broader organizational oversight and then connects audit work to major regulatory drivers such as GDPR, HIPAA, PCI DSS, and SOX. It also introduces risk appetite, risk tolerance, board responsibilities, and the need to link findings to the organization’s risk framework.

### 3. Audit Frameworks and Standards
The module covers the leading cybersecurity frameworks used in audit engagements, including NIST CSF, ISO 27001, ISO 27002, COBIT, and CIS Controls. It explains the NIST CSF functions, ISO 27001 structure and certification model, ISO 27002 control guidance, COBIT governance objectives, and CIS Controls as practical implementation guidance. It also explains how to select the right framework based on regulatory requirements, industry practice, maturity, certification needs, and available resources.

### 4. Scoping and Planning the Audit
This section focuses on the pre-engagement work that determines audit success. It covers scope definition, objectives, stakeholder interviews, engagement letters, timelines, audit charters, and resource planning. The module emphasizes that scope must be clearly documented, exclusions must be justified, and no fieldwork should begin without formal agreement and a realistic plan.

### 5. Technical Control Assessment
The module explains how to audit technical controls across network security, access management, endpoint protection, encryption, patch management, vulnerability management, logging, monitoring, and SIEM. It stresses that auditors must not only confirm control existence but also test whether those controls are configured correctly, consistently applied, and effective over time.

### 6. Administrative and Physical Controls
This section covers the non-technical safeguards that support the security program, including security policies, acceptable use, access control, incident response, change management, security awareness training, HR lifecycle controls, physical access controls, environmental safeguards, and incident response preparedness. It highlights that policies must be current, communicated, enforced, and supported by real evidence of operation.

### 7. Evidence Collection and Testing
The module explains the types of audit evidence, including direct observation, system-generated evidence, documentary evidence, third-party confirmation, re-performance, and inquiry. It also covers sampling methods, sample sizing, control testing techniques, automated audit tools, interviewing best practices, and the structure of working papers that support defensible audit conclusions.

### 8. Risk Assessment and Findings
This chapter covers risk rating methodologies, likelihood and impact analysis, finding classification, root cause analysis, and recurring vulnerability patterns. It explains qualitative and quantitative approaches, the use of standard risk frameworks, the need to avoid rating pressure from management, and the importance of identifying the underlying cause rather than only the visible symptom.

### 9. Reporting and Remediation
The module teaches how to write a professional audit report with an executive summary, scope, methodology, findings, management responses, and appendices. It presents the CCCER structure for findings and explains how different audiences use the report differently. It also covers remediation tracking, escalation, risk acceptance, re-testing, and closure, with an emphasis on independent verification before closing findings.

### 10. Continuous Improvement and Advanced Topics
The final chapter moves beyond periodic audits into continuous assurance and advanced audit domains. It covers continuous monitoring, audit automation, cloud security auditing, the shared responsibility model, vendor and third-party risk, and emerging areas such as AI, IoT, and supply-chain threats. The module shows that modern auditing must extend beyond annual reviews and adapt to changing technology and risk landscapes.

## Overall Learning Outcome
This module built a complete and practical foundation in cybersecurity auditing. It developed the full audit mindset from scope and evidence through classification, reporting, remediation, and continuous improvement, while also extending into cloud, vendor, and emerging-threat considerations that are now essential in modern audit practice.
